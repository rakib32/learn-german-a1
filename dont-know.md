# 🇩🇪 German Essentials: "I Don't Know" & Pronouns

A concise guide to common German phrases for uncertainty and a breakdown of first-person pronouns.

---

## 🤷 How to say "I don't know"

There are two primary ways to express a lack of knowledge in German, depending on the context and intensity.

### 1. Ich weiß nicht
**The standard and safest way.**

* **Literal Meaning:** "I know not."
* **Word-for-Word:**
    * `Ich` = I
    * `weiß` = know (from *wissen* → to know a fact)
    * `nicht` = not
* **Usage:** Neutral and polite. Works in both formal and informal situations. Use this when you genuinely lack specific information.
* **Example:** > *Kommst du heute?* (Are you coming today?)  
    > → **Ich weiß nicht.** (I don’t know.)

### 2. Ich habe keine Ahnung
**"I have no idea."**

* **Literal Meaning:** "I have no clue."
* **Word-for-Word:**
    * `Ich` = I
    * `habe` = have
    * `keine` = no / none
    * `Ahnung` = idea / clue
* **Usage:** More informal and "stronger." It suggests a complete lack of knowledge.
* **Example:**
    > *Wo ist er?* (Where is he?)  
    > → **Ich habe keine Ahnung.** (I have no idea.)

> [!TIP]
> **Tone Tip:** In professional or formal settings, stick to *Ich weiß nicht*.

## 👤 German Pronouns: ich / mich / mir

German pronouns change based on their role in the sentence (the "Case"). 

| German | English | Case | Role |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **ich** | I | Nominative | The Subject (doing the action) |
| **mich** | me | Accusative | The Direct Object (receiving the action) |
| **mir** | to/for me | Dative | The Indirect Object (affected by the action) |

### 1. ich — I (Nominative)
Use `ich` when you are the one performing the action.
* *Ich arbeite hier.* (I work here.)
* *Ich lerne Deutsch.* (I am learning German.)

### 2. mich — me (Accusative)
Use `mich` when the action is being done **to** you. (Ask: *Whom?*)
* *Er sieht mich.* (He sees me.)
* *Kannst du mich hören?* (Can you hear me?)

### 3. mir — to me / for me (Dative)
Used when something is done for your benefit or affects you. (Ask: *To/For whom?*)
* *Er hilft mir.* (He helps me.)
* *Gib mir das Buch.* (Give me the book.)

## 🧠 Memory Aids

* **The Rule of Thumb:** * `ich` → **I** do the action.
    * `mich` → The action **hits** me.
    * `mir` → The action **benefits** me.
* **The Multi-Case Sentence:**
    > *Ich gebe dir das Buch, weil es mir gefällt.* > (I give you the book because I like it / it pleases me.)
